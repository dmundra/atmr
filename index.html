Introduction

Design of visual interfaces is informed by a rich research literature on visual information processing. Much of what we know about reading and visual search was obtained from experiments that track eye motion, using special eye-tracking equipment. Design of good non-visual interfaces for visually-impaired users should likewise be informed by as much knowledge as we can gather about how non-visual interfaces are actually used. Our purpose in building a finger tracker is to provide research infrastructure to build knowledge of how printed tactile maps are used by visually-impaired users, so that knowledge can be used in exploring designs of other, more dynamic non-visual map interfaces such as those employing sound, haptics (touch), and proprioceptive (limb position) sensory channels.

We know of only two recent and ongoing projects elsewhere to construct finger tracking instruments, with aims similar to ours but focused on understanding how visually-impaired users read braille printed text. Our target domain is map reading. Printed tactile maps use bumps much like braille, but unlike braille text, the symbols on a map are arranged in a two-dimensional space that can be explored in different patterns. In fact, the pattern of exploration, and how or whether it differs with the map-reading task, are exactly the sort of data we wish to obtain. Assistive cartography researchers have observed these patterns in an informal manner, but have lacked the tools to produce and analyze quantitative data.

The finger tracker instrument itself will go through a series of incremental prototypes in consultation with Professor Amy Lobben, whose research includes production and use of tactile maps. A team of students in CIS 423, Software Methodology II at University of Oregon, produced an initial prototype. Our purposes in this prototype were to evaluate technical approaches to finger tracking in this domain, uncover issues and problems we might not discover without actually constructing the software, provide a concrete reference point for requirements and design discussions with Professor Lobben, and provide a modular skeleton system that will allow future student teams to revise or replace particular components without having to build every part from scratch. We believe we succeeded in each of these aims.
